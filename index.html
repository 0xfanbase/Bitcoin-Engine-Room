This rail sweeps once for every new block that actually arrives, and otherwise creeps only very slightly between them -- its position is roughly how much of the ~10-minute average block interval has elapsed since the last one. A second read on the same clock the block-height odometer runs on, not a separate metric.

Block height counts every block Bitcoin has ever mined, in order — the number shown is block #N since the very first block in 2009. A new block arrives roughly every 10 minutes on average, and it's the clock the halving countdown runs on.

Blocks to halving

The number of blocks left to mine before the mining reward is cut in half again — this happens automatically every 210,000 blocks, roughly every four years. It's a countdown built into the protocol itself, not an estimate.

Source: computed live from the current block height

Est. halving date

A calendar-date estimate for when block 1,050,000 will be mined, based on the network's current average block time. Because block times vary block to block, this date is a moving estimate, not a fixed appointment.

Source: computed from live block height + Bitcoin's fixed block-reward schedule

Gauges

What do the status colors mean?

LIVE — streaming in real time over a live connection to the fastest available source.

DELAYED — still real, current data, just pulled over a slower polled connection, or refreshed once a day rather than continuously.

STALE — every live source failed; you're looking at the last successfully saved snapshot, with its age shown, not a live number.

Difficulty · Retarget

—
—
—

Difficulty is a self-adjusting number that keeps new blocks arriving roughly every 10 minutes, no matter how much mining power joins or leaves — it retargets automatically every 2,016 blocks, about two weeks. Rising difficulty means more miners are competing for the reward.

Source: mempool.space (live) · blockchain.info (fallback)

Fees (sat/vB)

—
fastest · 30min · economy
—

What you pay, in satoshis per virtual byte, to get a transaction into the next block. The three tiers shown are the fee levels needed to hit fastest / 30-minute / economy confirmation times — fees rise and fall with how crowded the mempool is.

Source: mempool.space (live only, no fallback)

Mempool

—
pending transactions
—

The waiting room: every valid transaction that's been broadcast but not yet confirmed in a block sits here until a miner picks it up. A big mempool usually means the network is busy and fees are rising to compete for the next block's limited space.

Source: mempool.space, live WebSocket feed

Circulating Supply

How many bitcoin have been mined so far, out of the hard-capped 21,000,000 that will ever exist. New issuance is cut in half at every halving, so the pace of new coins entering circulation keeps shrinking on a fixed, published schedule.

Projections

Power Law Corridor

—

A long-running model, published by Giovanni Santostasi, that fits Bitcoin's price history to a power-law curve in log-log space — the idea is that price has grown at a roughly steady rate of deceleration over time.

The colored bands (Idle → Cruise → Redline) show where today's price sits relative to that long-run trend — but they're descriptive envelopes from historical fit residuals, not statistical confidence intervals, and not a prediction of where price goes next.

Source: model published by Giovanni Santostasi; refit nightly against this site's own historical price data

Model family published by Giovanni Santostasi. Bands are descriptive envelopes from historical fit residuals (±2σ in log10 space), not statistical confidence intervals. #power-law

4-Year Cycle Overlay

—

Stacks each Bitcoin halving cycle on top of the others, plotting % price performance against days since that cycle's own halving, so different cycles land on the same chart, comparably.

Bitcoin's four-year halving cycle has historically rhymed with big price cycles — this is a way to see whether the current cycle is tracking, lagging, or outrunning past ones, not a guarantee it keeps rhyming.

Source: computed from this site's own historical price data, sliced at each halving-epoch boundary

% performance vs. days since halving, each epoch normalized to its halving-day price. #cycle

Mayer Multiple · 200-Week MA

—

Mayer Multiple is today's price divided by its own 200-day moving average — 1.0 means price sits exactly at that average; well above means it's run up fast, well below means it's pulled back hard. The 200-week moving average is a much slower version of the same idea, using 200 weeks instead of 200 days.

Mayer Multiple has historically flagged overheated (high) or oversold (low) conditions; the 200-week average has historically acted as a long-term floor — price has rarely stayed below it for long.

Source: computed nightly from this site's own historical price data

Mayer Multiple = price / 200-day SMA. 200WMA = 200-week simple moving average. #mayer

What do these checks mean?

Every day this site runs six automated checks against its own data before publishing, rolling up to one overall result shown in the chip above: PASS (nothing found), WARN (worth a look, not urgent), or FAIL (a real problem). A findings list only appears above when a check flags something — a clean day shows none. Here's what each one actually looks for:

continuity — is there a gap in the daily history, a date with no saved snapshot?

cross_source_variance — do two independent price sources agree with each other today?

model_drift — did last night's price-model refit change suspiciously overnight? (Usually bad input data, not a broken market.)

staleness — how long has any gauge been showing old, non-live data instead of a fresh reading?

sanity_replay — re-checking the last month of saved data for impossible values, in case something slipped through the day it was saved.

site_integrity — is the site itself intact — no broken files, no missing pages, no runaway file sizes?
